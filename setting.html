Welcome to the Undying Earth

As the old earth's death rattle echoes in polluted lungs, empirical scientists and new-world artificers cooperate to develop a new life form, a self-sustaining, algaelike supraorganism which thrives off the ejecta of civilization and emits breathable air, pristine as starlight. The organism's spores burn at an unimaginable heat, forming a clean, recycled energy source harnessable by the same vast plants which once belched coal-smoke into the faltering sky.

In the old world, plagues licked through nations like brush fires; reactors failed and flooded and spilled their viscera into the greying ocean; the skies darkened with soot and war and with the death-stench of famine, failing crops, toxic waste, and decay. But here the air is clean. In only six years, the organism's proliferation has erased human damage once called irreversible, leaving only fast-vanishing industrial scars.

But with each generation of our biological savior come changes, adaptations, mutations. The spores the life-form releases fill the air invisibly, becoming as much a part of us as we of it. We wander the earth in a half-unwaking state, our collective dreams forming as much of a part of the horizon as the dirt and decay of our once-sprawling civilizations do. We build glittering, dreamlike cities that stretch far above the cloudline, the altitude clearing our heads and giving rise to powerful, aristocratic city-states, or we wander into the halls of now-alien boreal glades and forget our bodies ankle-deep in moss.

As the decades spin on, the airy isolation above and the reeling etherscapes below drift further and further apart. For those above, the surface is only a nightmare recalled in the panicked breaths before it fades—for those below, the cloud-cities are no more than slurred shadows against a brilliant spillway of stars.

This is the age of humanity, when we discover magic and learn to dream again.
